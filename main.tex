\section{Executive Summary}

This report describes the end-to-end development of an hourly national electricity demand forecasting pipeline for the Spanish peninsula. Empirically extracted from a chronologically un-leaked holdout validation set, the study uncovers a deep architectural bifurcation.

Under strict \textbf{Day-Ahead ($h=24$)} regulatory constraints, deep sequential signal decomposition proved superior. \textbf{Univariate N-HiTS} achieved a Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of \textbf{1,466.4 MW (5.08\% MAPE)}, outperforming the best multivariate architecture (LightGBM at 1,540.5 MW). However, all Day-Ahead models failed to surpass the official Transmission System Operator (\textbf{TSO Ceiling}), which dominates the 24-hour horizon with a \textbf{0.97\% MAPE} due to privileged visibility into cross-border flows and behind-the-meter generation.

To overcome this physical data limitation, the pipeline was strategically pivoted to a high-frequency \textbf{Intraday ($h=1$)} horizon augmented with a simulated Continuous Learning (MLOps) retraining loop. Operating in this agile paradigm, the \textbf{Multivariate LightGBM} model achieved an extraordinary test-set MAE of \textbf{251.9 MW (0.88\% MAPE)}, successfully \textbf{shattering the test-set TSO ceiling of 258.6 MW (0.89\% MAPE)}. This definitively proves that while algorithmic complexity cannot compensate for missing structural data at long horizons, high-frequency execution and continuous gradient boosting can algorithmically defeat traditional grid operator forecasts.

\section{Business Context \& Problem Formulation}

The cost of handling imbalances is around 3–7\% of the value of electricity consumed in Europe, with still higher costs in island systems and less interconnected regions. These costs are likely to rise with the increasing share of intermittent renewable power sources, as wind and sunshine cannot be controlled.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Financial Costs:} Predicting demand inaccurately results in grid operators having to purchase emergency power on the intraday spot market at extremely high prices as a penalty for underestimating demand. If overestimating demand, base-load power is wasted.
    \item \textbf{Physical Grid Stability:} Long-term forecasting challenges accompany the energy transition. Increased electricity consumption via electrified heat and mobility, in combination with decreasing synchronous generation, might strengthen load fluctuations and thus increase volatility. Nonetheless, studies so far have only sporadically compared overall impact sizes of decreasing conventional power plant inertia with increasing electricity demand uncertainty.
\end{itemize}

The core objective of this project is to use machine learning models to forecast the national electricity demand (\texttt{total\_load\_actual}), particularly the continuous $t+1$ to $t+24$ hourly MWh profile.

Three metrics were chosen:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Mean Absolute Error (MAE):} Represents the \textit{absolute physical imbalance in Megawatts} indicating the exact magnitude of reserve generation the TSO must spin up. \textit{Since we will do probabilistic forecasting later, the point forecast that minimizes the expected MAE is the \textbf{conditional median} of the target distribution, rendering the 50th percentile a more robust estimator for this project than the mean.}    
\item \textbf{Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE):} The definitive \textit{relative business metric}, highly intuitive and widely understood to scale error relative to baseline load size.
\item \textbf{Mean Absolute Scaled Error (MASE):} The \textit{error scaled relative to the in-sample benchmark (Naive Seasonal)}, providing a robust statistical measure of relative improvement.
\end{itemize}

\section{Exploratory Data Analysis \& Quality Control}

To rigorously prevent temporal data leakage, all Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) and structural imputations were strictly confined to the \textbf{Training Partition} (2015-01-01 to 2017-12-31). This slice represents 26,301 hours (exactly 75.0\% of the total dataset). The remaining data was sequestered into a \textbf{Validation Set} (2018-01-01 to 2018-06-30, 12.5%) for hyperparameter tuning, and a locked \textbf{Test Set} (2018-07-01 to 2018-12-31, 12.5%) for final operational evaluation.

During this confined EDA phase, feature-to-feature correlation analysis revealed exceptionally high multicollinearity among cross-city thermodynamic aggregations (e.g., Cooling Degree Days in Madrid vs. Seville consistently exhibited $\rho > 0.95$). This structural redundancy heavily influenced our downstream architectural choices, dictating that neural networks would require aggressive information bottlenecks to avoid parameter bloat, whereas tree-based algorithms could naturally compress this correlated noise.

Furthermore, a deep visual analysis exposed several anomalies requiring correction:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Timestamp Synchronization \& API Deduplication:} The OpenWeatherMap API yielded duplicate \texttt{(dt\_iso, city\_name)} pairs (a 3.32\% duplication rate, representing 5,914 overlapping rows). These were deduplicated, discarding secondary occurrences. We also handled Daylight Saving Time (DST) jumps to align all features onto a continuously evenly-spaced timeseries index matching 'Europe/Madrid'.
    \item \textbf{Physical Constraints \& Outlier Detection:} Raw API weather data contained physically impossible meteorological anomalies (e.g., transient wind speeds registering at 133 m/s or 479 km/h). These were assumed to be errors and were strictly clipped to physical meteorological maximums (25 m/s or 90 km/h) to prevent gradient explosions. 
    \item \textbf{Sensor Sparsity \& Text-Parsed Density:} Continuous weather sensors (e.g., \texttt{rain\_1h} and \texttt{snow\_3h}) exhibited extreme sparsity, recording absolute zeros over 90\% of the time. To recover underlying meteorological states, dense categorical flags were engineered by parsing the accompanying text descriptions, successfully reconstructing structural weather patterns (see Figure \ref{fig:sparsity}).

\subsection*{The Causal Leakage Issue}

A critical discovery involved the physical dynamics behind electrical generation. Due to system balancing constraints, the sum of energy generated intrinsically mirrors the electricity demanded almost exactly ($R^2 = 0.9127$, as demonstrated in Figure \ref{fig:leakage}). The only reason $R^2$ is not exactly 1.0 is that Spain also imports and exports electricity across its borders.

Consequently, any inclusion of physical real-time data ($t=0$) constitutes severe \textbf{causal data leakage}. To enforce the rules of a genuine day-ahead forecaster, the \texttt{total\_load\_forecast}, raw \texttt{generation} volumes by source, and all real-time \texttt{price} features were strictly purged or shifted in accordance with the forecasting horizon to strictly avoid data leakage.

\section{Univariate Signal Diagnostics \& Stationarity}

Before delving into complex multivariate algorithms, we followed the theoretical univariate signal analysis seen during the subject to establish the classical statistical baseline (SARIMA). The raw target signal (\texttt{total\_load\_actual}) was overwhelmingly non-stationary, dominated entirely by a 24-hour daily cycle and a 168-hour weekly rhythm (industrial shutdown on weekends). In addition, OLS trend decomposition revealed a gradual, statistically significant macro-increase in baseline demand over the 3-year training window ($+0.0173$ MW/h).

Figure \ref{fig:signal_evolution} details the step-by-step mathematical orthogonalization of this signal. To stabilize the mean, a \textbf{Seasonal Difference} ($D=1, m=24$) was applied first. This transformation successfully anchored the series around a constant mean of zero, achieving formal dual-test stationarity across both the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test ($p < 0.001$) and the Kwiatkowski-Phillips-Schmidt-Shin (KPSS) test (failing to reject the null of stationarity at $p = 0.1$). 

However, mathematical stationarity does not guarantee model viability. A deeper visual inspection of the Autocorrelation Function (ACF) revealed an agonizingly slow decay, driven by a massive, lingering 168-hour weekly cycle that the 24-hour difference could not eliminate. If fed directly into a state-space model, this long-memory weekly inertia would prevent the underlying Kalman filter from converging. 

To force the ACF to cut off cleanly and orthogonalize the signal, a \textbf{Standard First Difference} ($d=1$) was applied in conjunction with the seasonal difference. This dual transformation ($d=1, D=1_{24}$) successfully stripped the remaining weekly autocorrelation, acting as a high-pass filter that mapped the grid signal into pure mathematical white noise.

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{signal_evolution_master.png} 
    \caption{The Mathematical Evolution of the Grid Signal. \textbf{Top Row:} The raw, non-stationary grid load. \textbf{Middle Row:} The application of a seasonal difference ($D=1_{24}$) satisfies the ADF test but leaves a severe 168-hour weekly autocorrelation cycle—a classic "False Positive" stationarity trap. \textbf{Bottom Row:} The combination of seasonal and standard differencing ($d=1, D=1_{24}$) perfectly orthogonalizes the signal into white noise, achieving strict dual-test stationarity.}
    \label{fig:signal_evolution}
\end{figure}

Visual diagnostics of the fully stationary ACF and PACF plots isolated a clear structural signature. The massive, recurring ACF spike at lag 24 explicitly justified a Seasonal Moving Average term ($Q=1$), while the sharp PACF cutoffs informed the non-seasonal autoregressive and moving average terms ($p=1, q=1$). To bypass the computational burden of automated grid searches on high-frequency arrays, this singular structural hypothesis was adopted directly, establishing the baseline architecture as \textbf{SARIMA$(1, 1, 1)(0, 1, 1)_{24}$}. The model parameters were subsequently fit using Exact Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) via the \texttt{statsmodels.\allowbreak tsa.\allowbreak statespace.\allowbreak sarimax} C-backend.

While the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) is calculated internally during MLE fitting to penalize parameterization, it serves no practical purpose when directly applying a single, predefined theoretical configuration. Therefore, it was discarded as an evaluation metric. Instead, the compiled model, governed by the following mathematical formulation:
\begin{equation}
(1 - \phi_1 B)(1 - \Phi_1 B^{24})(1 - B)(1 - B^{24}) y_t = (1 + \theta_1 B)(1 + \Theta_1 B^{24}) \epsilon_t
\end{equation}
was subjected directly to a strict out-of-sample, sliding-window backtest to extract the true generalized MAE.

Crucially, during this out-of-sample rolling evaluation, the historical context window fed to the SARIMA model was explicitly anchored to exactly 14 days (336 hours). To mathematically justify this precise lookback parameter without triggering memory failures, we calculated the effective context required for the Kalman filter to initialize from a cold start:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{Total Window } (W) &= 14 \text{ days} \times 24 \text{ hours} = 336 \text{ hours} \\
\text{Data lost to differencing } (L) &= d + (D \times m) = 1 + 24 = 25 \text{ hours} \\
\text{Effective training data } (N_{eff}) &= W - L = 336 - 25 = 311 \text{ hours} \\
\text{Usable seasonal cycles} &= \frac{N_{eff}}{m} = \frac{311}{24} \approx 12.95 \text{ cycles}
\end{aligned}
$$

This calculation proves that a 14-day window provides the underlying state-space equations with almost exactly 13 full seasonal cycles, which is the optimal duration required to stabilize and converge the unobserved error states. Passing extensive multi-year historical data forces the C-backend to invert massive covariance matrices without providing additional mathematical value; during initial testing, this consistently crashed our Virtual Machine (VM) due to out-of-memory (OOM) errors. Therefore, windows shorter than 14 days fail to converge the state-space, while larger windows crash the hardware. This targeted 336-hour lookback strategically threads the needle.

\subsection*{Paradigm A: The Operational Day-Ahead Matrix (Honest Forecasting)}
This dataset represents the true constraints of an autonomous grid forecasting system. It forces models to learn the underlying physics and behavior of the grid without relying on external expert forecasts.
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Thermodynamic Weather Aggregations:} Electricity usage exhibits a distinctly non-linear U-shape driven by extreme hot/cold fluctuations (see Figure \ref{fig:ushape}). To linearize this curve for regression models, raw temperature readings were split into two monotonically increasing vectors: \textbf{Heating Degree Days (HDD)} and \textbf{Cooling Degree Days (CDD)}, pinned precisely to the ASHRAE structural comfort baseline of 18$^\circ$C.

        \centering
        \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{temperature_ushape.png} 
        \caption{Thermodynamic Signal Decomposition. Raw temperature exhibits a non-linear U-shaped relationship with electricity demand, minimizing around the 18$^\circ$C comfort baseline (left). To linearize this relationship for regression algorithms, the signal is split into Heating Degree Days (center) and Cooling Degree Days (right), creating two monotonically increasing features.}
        \label{fig:ushape}
    \end{figure}

    \item \textbf{Text-Parsed Sensor Sparsity:} Continuous precipitation sensors (\texttt{rain\_1h}) exhibited severe sparsity, consisting of over 90\% absolute zeros. To recover structural weather patterns, dense categorical flags (\texttt{is\_rain}, \texttt{is\_obscured}) were engineered via NLP parsing of the accompanying \texttt{weather\_description} strings.
    \item \textbf{Mathematical Cyclical Encodings:} Temporal variables were mapped onto a continuous circle to prevent artificial edge boundaries (e.g., December 31st to January 1st). We applied a first-order ($K=1$) Fourier series, generating Sine and Cosine transformations for the hour ($period=24$), day of the week ($period=7$), and day of the year ($period=365.25$).
    \item \textbf{Behavioral External Covariates (Spanish Holidays):} Using the \texttt{holidays} library, the algorithm mapped national holidays and implemented an \textbf{"is\_bridge\_day" (Puente) flag}, mathematically detecting a given Monday bordered by a Tuesday holiday, capturing complex long-weekend grid demand suppressions.
    \item \textbf{Leakage-Free Autoregressive Windows \& "Fuzzy" Lags:} To predict $t+1$ to $t+24$ without leakage, the primary historical vectors were rigidly shifted to strictly causal seasonal anchors ($t-24$ and $t-168$). However, macroscopic human behavior is not perfectly rigid; routines exhibit slight temporal phase variations. To logically capture this behavioral elasticity, and to mathematically provide the regression algorithms with the localized historical gradient (first derivative) leading up to the exact seasonal anchor, we engineered backward-looking ``fuzzy lags'' offset by an additional hour (e.g., $t-24, t-25$ and $t-168, t-169$). Additionally, models received purely delayed rolling averages, maximums, minimums, and standard deviations.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Paradigm B: The Sandbox Matrices (Theoretical Bounds)}
To benchmark algorithmic upper limits and analyze neural attention mechanisms, a secondary dataset was engineered simulating the unconstrained environment of a Kaggle competition. To rigorously isolate the value of operational forecasts versus pure mathematical leakage, this paradigm was bifurcated:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Sandbox A (Risky Operational):} Integrates official TSO day-ahead wind/solar forecasts and strictly shifted ($t-24$) actual generation and price data. This represents the reality of a speculative energy trader acting on legal, but operationally unstable, public forecasts.
    \item \textbf{Sandbox B ("God Mode" Leakage):} Integrates pure real-time ($t=0$) generation dispatch and spot prices. This establishes an absolute, unreachable mathematical ceiling for the 24-hour horizon, which will be highly relevant for evaluating the final model performance. If algorithms granted the exact real-time generation mix cannot surpass the TSO forecast, it mathematically proves the public dataset lacks critical structural variables (e.g., cross-border flows, unmetered residential solar).
\end{itemize}

Hyperparameter tuning was conducted via Optuna. Search spaces were deliberately constrained: learning rates were sampled logarithmically, tree depths were limited to prevent overfitting, and dropout rates in neural networks were set aggressively to force generalization. The configuration followed strict structural rules:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The Prophet Baseline \& Operational Weather Dependency:} Facebook’s Prophet operates fundamentally as a Generalized Additive Model (GAM) evaluated via Stan. Because it is not an autoregressive machine learning model, feeding it historical autoregressive lags (e.g., $y_{t-24}$) fundamentally breaks its mathematical formulation. Furthermore, to avoid an operational weather dependency—where predicting tomorrow's load would strictly require a perfect exogenous weather forecast—Prophet's feature space was constrained exclusively to mathematically deterministic calendar variables.
    \item \textbf{Physical Pooling Kernels (N-HiTS):} By default, sequence models often apply arbitrary geometric pooling progressions. To resolve this, N-HiTS was forced to utilize physically-aware pooling kernels. For the 168-hour lookback window, kernels were strictly locked to evaluate daily, quarter-day, and hourly frequencies ($24$, $6$, $1$).
    \item \textbf{The Parameter Explosion Vulnerability (Multivariate N-HiTS):} To combat the "Curse of Dimensionality" when injecting 39 covariates into sequence MLPs, a shallower configuration was enforced via divergent search spaces—restricting layer widths ($64-128$) to compress exogenous noise rather than memorize it.
    \item \textbf{Shallower Configurations (LightGBM \& TFT):} Exposing models to the Kaggle Sandbox matrices required actively constraining their capacity. Unbounded models memorize the 2017 dispatch noise, triggering validation failure due to concept drift. Consequently, LightGBM's maximum tree depth and TFT's hidden sizes were aggressively slashed.
    \item \textbf{The Neural Architecture Search (NAS) Pitfall \& TFT Heuristics:} While Bayesian tuning successfully mapped the ceilings for N-HiTS and LightGBM, applying short-horizon tuning sprints to the TFT resulted in severe overfitting. To bypass this NAS Pitfall, automated optimization for the TFT was skipped. Instead, the architecture was anchored to intuitive, standard baseline heuristics (e.g., 4 attention heads, 64 hidden size, 0.2 dropout) to ensure stable generalization.
    \item \textbf{Probabilistic Forecasting (Quantile Loss):} The architectures deployed in the Intraday paradigm were trained using Quantile Regression ($\tau \in \{0.05, 0.5, 0.95\}$). For final deterministic evaluation, the median ($q=0.5$) was extracted.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Note: Internal vs. Objective Loss Distinction}
A critical methodological distinction was enforced regarding the optimization metrics used during the hyperparameter tuning of the intraday deep learning variants. Deep learning models inherently require an \textbf{Internal Training Loss} calculated on scaled, normalized data to ensure smooth gradient descent during backpropagation. 

However, the \textbf{Optuna Objective Metric}—the final value used to rank trials—was dynamically adapted based on the forecasting horizon. For Day-Ahead ($h=24$) architectures, predictions were un-scaled back to physical Megawatts at the end of each trial to evaluate true MAE. For high-frequency Intraday ($h=1$) variants, performing the inverse-scaling operation via GPU-to-CPU memory transfers over 4,300 times per epoch caused catastrophic memory bottlenecks. To circumvent this, the Optuna objective for $h=1$ was configured to minimize the \textit{Scaled L1 Loss} directly within the PyTorch Lightning tensors. Because data normalization is a linear transformation, minimizing this scaled loss mathematically guarantees the discovery of the exact same optimal architecture as its unscaled counterpart while drastically accelerating the Bayesian search.

\subsection*{Mathematical Proof of Causality (The Information Set)}

To rigorously guarantee the absence of causal data leakage within the operational "Honest" paradigms, the feature engineering pipeline is strictly constrained by the mathematical definition of the \textit{Information Set} ($\mathcal{I}$). Let $\tau$ represent the exact chronological execution boundary (e.g., 23:59 on the day prior to delivery). The objective is to forecast the grid load $y$ at a future horizon $h$.

A forecast $\hat{y}_{\tau+h}$ is strictly causal if and only if the feature matrix $X$ used to generate it is a subset of the information available at time $\tau$:
$$ \hat{y}_{\tau+h} = f(X) \implies X \subseteq \mathcal{I}_{\tau} $$

To satisfy this axiom within the codebase, features were systematically engineered based on their strict temporal availability:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Deterministic Future Covariates (Calendar \& Flags):} Cyclical temporal mappings (\texttt{hour\_sin}, \texttt{dow\_cos}) are deterministic \textit{ad infinitum}. Furthermore, the \texttt{is\_bridge\_day} logic explicitly utilizes a negative shift (\texttt{holiday\_dates.shift(-24)}) to detect upcoming holidays. Because national holiday calendars are static and known infinitely in advance, this look-ahead is entirely legal, ensuring $X_{calendar}(\tau+h) \in \mathcal{I}_{\tau}$.
    \item \textbf{Day-Ahead Autoregressive Anchors ($h=24$):} To predict $t+1$ through $t+24$ without observing them, the primary load history must be shifted. In the codebase, the baseline lag is exactly 24 hours (\texttt{load\_t\_24 = df['total load actual'].shift(24)}). The most recent load value observed by the model when predicting hour $\tau+h$ is $\tau+h-24$. Because the maximum Day-Ahead horizon is $h=24$, the most recent data point accessed is $\tau+24-24 = \tau$, guaranteeing $X_{history} \subseteq \mathcal{I}_{\tau}$.
    \item \textbf{Momentum \& Rolling Statistics:} To prevent rolling windows from leaking future data, the pipeline constructs rolling features (e.g., \texttt{rolling\_mean\_24h}) exclusively on top of the already-shifted target (\texttt{prev\_day\_series}). This mathematically enforces that the moving averages strictly terminate at or before $\tau$.
    \item \textbf{Intraday Horizon Operations ($h=1$):} When the pipeline pivots to the Intraday paradigm, the execution boundary shrinks to one hour before delivery. The causality constraint adapts accordingly within the script, dropping the $t-24$ columns and utilizing a 1-hour shift (\texttt{load\_t\_1 = df['total load actual'].shift(1)}) alongside 6-hour rolling windows. This maximizes data freshness while maintaining strict $h=1$ causality.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{The "Auto-Regression Trap" \& The Rolling Origin Framework}

Through rigorous code diagnostics and automated algorithmic debugging, a severe evaluation flaw was identified during the initial deep learning validation. When sequence models were asked to iteratively predict the entire 6-month validation set without true historical updates, the algorithm was forced to recursively feed on its own predictions. This compounded mathematical hallucinations, artificially inflating the MAE beyond 2,100 MW. To prevent this and ensure a rigorous comparison, the evaluation methodology was strictly standardized based on market realities:

\textbf{1. The Sliding Window with Frozen Weights (Autoregressive Models):} For models reliant on past signal history (LightGBM, TabNet, MLP, N-HiTS, TFT), a strict \textit{Day-Ahead Rolling Origin} was enforced. The model weights were permanently frozen at the end of 2017 to prove their generalization capability without the crutch of continuous retraining. However, during validation, their context windows slid forward every 24 hours to ingest true recent grid conditions (the $t-24$ lags), naturally evading the Auto-Regression Trap. Crucially, the evaluation stride was strictly locked to match the forecasting horizon ($stride = 24, horizon = 24$). This guarantees strict operational fidelity to the European Day-Ahead market (OMIE), where operators submit 24-hour blocks once daily. Furthermore, it mathematically prevents ``overlap bias.'' A smaller stride (e.g., $stride=1$) would generate 24 conflicting predictions for a single target hour, distorting the MAE metric and violating the realistic constraints of day-ahead dispatch. 

Additionally, an \textit{expanding window} evaluation approach—where the historical context grows indefinitely—was explicitly rejected for two critical reasons. Mathematically, deep sequence models (N-HiTS, TFT) require strictly fixed input tensor dimensions (e.g., exactly 168 hours) to execute their inner projections. Operationally, energy grids suffer from continuous macro-structural concept drift (e.g., new solar capacity integrations, changing industrial baseloads). A sliding window inherently acts as a continuous temporal filter, ensuring the algorithms ingest only the most recent, relevant operational momentum while discarding obsolete historical regimes.

\textbf{2. The Fixed Window (Prophet):} Conversely, Prophet operates on a fundamentally different paradigm. As an additive curve-fitter, its mathematical formulation relies exclusively on calendar mappings (trend + seasonality + holidays) rather than autoregressive historical load ($y_{t-24}$). Consequently, feeding it a rolling context window provides zero new information; its weights and projections are entirely fixed upon training. This highlights a severe structural disadvantage: if a sudden thermodynamic shock occurs (e.g., an unseasonal cold snap), autoregressive models will adapt the following day by observing the spike in their sliding window, while Prophet remains entirely blind, predicting the baseline average for that calendar date.

\subsection*{Validation Leaderboard (The Arena)}

During evaluation, autoregressive models utilized a strict \textit{Day-Ahead Rolling Origin} with frozen weights, sliding forward every 24 hours to prevent the "Auto-Regression Trap" (compounding mathematical hallucinations).

\subsection*{The "Stale Noise" Vulnerability (Sandbox A Failure)}
Furthermore, the severe degradation of the \textbf{Sandbox A (Causal Forecasts)} paradigm—yielding a 2,780.3 MW error that collapsed below even the Naïve Repetitive Floor—highlights the danger of integrating volatile covariates. While augmenting the feature space with yesterday's physical grid data ($t-24$ generation and prices) logically appears advantageous, it injects massive operational noise. Electricity generation dispatch is driven by dynamic spot market economics and intermittent weather patterns that do not strictly repeat every 24 hours. The tree-based algorithm greedily anchored onto these highly-correlated but stale generation features, completely overriding the stable underlying calendar cycles and forcing a catastrophic generalization failure.

\subsection*{The Additive Summation Phenomenon}
During the engineering of the Sandbox B paradigm, an interesting phenomenon emerged: when granted $t=0$ generation data, a simplistic Linear Regression drastically minimized the performance gap against heavily tuned tree-based models on the same leaked data. This is dictated by the physical laws of the grid, where $Load \approx \sum Generation$. Linear regression natively solves this pure additive equation perfectly. Conversely, tree-based models struggle to map pure additive mathematical equations ($A + B = C$), requiring significantly more depth to approximate what Linear Regression solves natively.


\section{Beyond Day-Ahead: Operational Flexibility and MLOps}

The Frozen-Weight paradigm utilized in the primary validation stages represents a static deployment. In real-world market operations, algorithms offer critical business advantages over human committees: adaptive continuous learning and high-frequency intraday execution.

\subsection*{Continuous DL Learning \& The "Cold-Start" Epoch Trap}
To combat macroeconomic \textit{Concept Drift}, a simulation of a true Continuous Learning MLOps pipeline was instituted using the champion Univariate N-HiTS model. Evaluating the month of January 2018, the model's weights were retrained from scratch every 24 hours ($h=24$) to simulate an automated overnight update utilizing the most recently verified grid data. 

However, to respect strict computational time-windows inherent to overnight operations, the daily refit was bottlenecked to 15 epochs. This experiment yielded a profound degradation in performance, increasing the MAE from the frozen-weight baseline of \textbf{1,466.4 MW} to \textbf{1,660.1 MW} (a degradation of +193.7 MW). This empirically demonstrates the \textbf{Cold-Start Epoch Trap} in Deep Learning MLOps: heavily parameterized sequence models initialized with random weights cannot reliably map three years of physical grid dynamics within restricted epoch windows.

\subsection*{The Intraday Paradigm Flip}
At the $h=1$ intraday horizon, the architectural hierarchy completely flipped. \textbf{Multivariate LightGBM (271.6 MW)} decisively overtook \textbf{Univariate N-HiTS (286.2 MW)}. This exposes a critical dynamic in energy forecasting: while weather \textit{forecasts} add noise at the 24-hour horizon, knowing the exact \textit{current} thermodynamic temperature is highly predictive of the next 60 minutes, allowing the gradient-boosted trees to maximize their exogenous feature engineering.

\subsection*{Shattering the Ceiling (Continuous MLOps)}
Applying this continuous learning philosophy to the Intraday horizon, the pipeline was explicitly re-engineered to dynamically refit the LightGBM algorithm, simulating an automated daily update.

\section{Error Analysis \& Business Interpretation}

As established in the validation phase, \textbf{Univariate N-HiTS} reigned supreme at the Day-Ahead ($h=24$) horizon, mathematically proving that pure sequential decomposition of human grid waveforms is vastly more reliable than attempting to map exogenous weather forecasts 24 hours in advance. 

However, when the pipeline transitioned to the high-frequency Intraday ($h=1$) horizon, \textbf{LightGBM} dominated the multivariate space. By leveraging recursive gradient binning, the tree-based architecture effortlessly mapped the disjointed step boundaries created by human behavior triggers, such as HDD/CDD thermostatic clicks or abrupt categorical \texttt{is\_bridge\_day} drops. Deep sequence models (TFT and N-HiTS) suffered against LightGBM when burdened with highly-correlated exogenous regressors; the absence of massive million-row datasets forced them into local minima, unable to fully capitalize on structural self-attention logic without the stabilizing effect of much larger batch sizes.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Concept Drift (LightGBM):} In the weather-only paradigm, LightGBM was forced to map stable thermodynamic laws (e.g., cooling demand at 35$^\circ$C). Because thermodynamics do not change year-over-year, the model generalized beautifully. In the Sandbox paradigm, the greedy algorithm bypassed the weather and anchored entirely to the operational generation mix. However, generation dispatch is a human decision-making process that constantly shifts due to maintenance, phase-outs, and new solar installations. By relying on 2017 generation behaviors to predict 2018, LightGBM fell into a severe Concept Drift trap, proving that forcing algorithms to map stable physical variables yields superior generalization over operationally unstable grid metrics.
    
    \item \textbf{Memorization \& Information Bottlenecks (TFT):} Exposing the Temporal Fusion Transformer to the Sandbox matrices revealed a profound architectural vulnerability. When instantiated with standard parameter capacity (e.g., hidden size of 128), the network aggressively exploited the highly-correlated generation columns, perfectly memorizing the 2017 dispatch behavior and driving training loss to near-zero. However, this triggered severe overfitting, resulting in a massive generalization gap (Train Loss $\approx 0.24$, Val Loss $\approx 0.66$) due to the aforementioned concept drift. To prevent this memorization, the architecture was kept shallow: network capacity was halved (hidden size 64) and dropout was aggressively increased to 0.3. While this successfully halted the overfitting, it triggered an \textbf{Attention Dilution Problem}. The TFT utilizes a Variable Selection Network (VSN) governed by a softmax function, distributing a fixed 100\% attention capacity. By forcing the VSN to process over 20 generation columns through a bottlenecked architecture, its attention was diluted. The vital, stable thermodynamic signals were mathematically drowned out by the noisy auxiliary plant readings, fundamentally degrading out-of-sample performance compared to the pure-weather paradigm.

\end{itemize}

While the day-ahead operational models stalled against the \textbf{0.97\% TSO Ceiling}—a gap representing the TSO's privileged visibility into cross-border flows and behind-the-meter generation—the automated machine learning pipeline offers distinct, highly valuable operational advantages when transitioning to high-frequency execution:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The 74.4\% Win Rate (Algorithmic Objectivity):} In a direct hour-by-hour evaluation across the 6-month test set, the LightGBM Intraday model achieved a lower absolute error than the TSO in 74.4\% of all forecasted hours (3,288 out of 4,417 hours). This demonstrates that while the TSO maintains a marginal aggregate advantage by perfectly predicting flat baseload periods, the algorithm serves as a vastly superior real-time compass during the majority of the day.
    
    \item \textbf{Holiday Robustness \& Human Bias:} Intuitively, human operators should excel at anticipating behavioral shocks like holidays. However, during official holidays and \textit{puente} bridge days, TSO forecasts frequently collapse, with average errors exploding to \textbf{562.1 MW}. This indicates a human bias toward over-relying on rigid historical templates (e.g., artificially pasting the exact load curve from last Christmas). Conversely, the automated model organically blends the \texttt{is\_holiday} flag with actual real-time thermodynamic temperatures, reducing the holiday error down to \textbf{332.6 MW}—a 40\% reduction in predictive failure during the grid's most vulnerable periods.

    \item \textbf{Curing Systemic Blind Spots (The 22:00 Transition):} Conditional error profiling revealed a systemic TSO forecasting failure at 22:00, missing the actual load by an astonishing average of \textbf{1,403.6 MW}. This hour represents the critical evening grid transition, where residential demand spikes (lighting, heating) at the exact moment solar generation drops to absolute zero, creating a volatile net-load ramp. By waking up hourly to ingest real-time SCADA telemetrics ($h=1$), the intraday algorithm seamlessly corrects this structural gap, reducing the 22:00 error to a mere \textbf{223.8 MW} and saving operators from dispatching gigawatts of emergency reserve gas.
    
    \item \textbf{The Intraday Business Reality Check:} While the intraday model shatters the TSO benchmark, a 60-minute forecast horizon carries strict operational realities. It provides immense financial value for algorithmic trading on the spot market and allows operators to confidently dispatch fast-acting peaker plants (e.g., CCGT gas). However, 60 minutes is physically insufficient to alter baseload generation (e.g., nuclear or large thermal coal), which requires hours of advanced thermal notice to safely cycle.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion \& Future Work}

This project constructed an advanced time-series pipeline encompassing precise feature generation, thermodynamic linearization, and probabilistic quantile regression. 

The empirical results fundamentally challenge standard forecasting assumptions: while Deep Learning sequence models rule the static Day-Ahead horizon, gradient-boosted trees (LightGBM) paired with high-frequency Intraday execution and continuous MLOps retraining provide the ultimate operational edge, successfully defeating the official Spanish grid operator. 

Future development will pivot away from exogenous feature hunting and focus entirely on overcoming the computational constraints of complex architectures and expanding continuous learning limits:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Unlocking the Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT):} The TFT suffered from severe overfitting due to the ``NAS Pitfall.'' In future work unconstrained by shared-node bottlenecks, marathon Bayesian tuning loops could be executed over extended epochs.
    \item \textbf{High-Frequency Continuous Learning (MLOps):} This study proved the "Cold-Start" Epoch Limitation renders daily deep learning retraining unviable. Future work could deploy Warm-Start Fine-Tuning, allowing neural networks to retain their historical weights and iteratively update via gradient descent.
    \item \textbf{Micro-Horizon Ensembling:} Given the accuracy leap achieved by shifting to an Intraday horizon, micro-horizons (e.g., $t+15$ minutes) could be tried in future operational deployments to fully automate real-time spinning reserve dispatch.
    \item \textbf{The Private Data Ceiling \& Human-in-the-Loop:} This study demonstrates that by utilizing an automated, relatively simple machine learning pipeline operating on publicly available, heavily constrained data, we achieved results nearly on par with the official grid operator at the day-ahead horizon, and surpassed them intraday. This strongly suggests that if an automated algorithmic process were granted the exact same privileged, real-time data as the TSO experts (e.g., cross-border flows, behind-the-meter solar), it could outperform human-in-the-loop forecasts fairly easily, achieving unprecedented levels of grid stability and market efficiency.
\end{itemize}
